Understanding how media:

1. Act upon us such that our
bodies become interfaces
(aka "affect").

2. Interlock sensation with story through
voluntary or involuntary imitation
(aka "mimetic communication").

Note that "using" (as in "using media") doesn't quite work here,
partly because affect does not belong to media. It's a relation.
(source for image in next slide)

What's affect?

a visceral force or vibe
we don't consciously recognize

the relation between affection (being affected)
and action (the capacity to affect)

an experience that precedes, exceeds,
or escapes language

Got goosebumps? Got fomo?

Affect is not emotion.

Emotions are states we recognize:
anger, disgust, fear, happiness,
sadness, and surprise, e.g.

Affects are the forces that
precede + inform emotions.

"Affect isn't what I feel,
[it's] what forces me to feel" (Shaviro).

Dan Siegel's "hand model" of the brain maps affect (which he calls emotion)
to the limbic area of the brain.

Ok, but this is a
Media Studies course.

Affect is an embodied social relation with media.

This relation produces a "shared pulse,"
where we voluntarily or involuntarily
imitate what we perceive with others
who are experiencing the same work
with us in some way.

Ok, this is still a
Media Studies course.

When I was a kid, I'd jump in my room
when Mario jumped on the screen.
Now I sit and just jump in my brain. #adulting

This "mimetic communication" involves
1) the desire to temporarily become
someone / something else, and
2) a social synchronization with others.

The horror genre, including the jump scare, is a space to witness
the shared pulse of affect and mimetic communication in cinema.
(screen of The Last of Us (next slide) care of Naughty Dog and PlayStation)

Consider a popular example.

Post-apocalyptic franchises like The Last of Us
mobilize affect by priming us for catastrophe.

Their force is a constant state of low-level anxiety.

This relation is called "premediation" (Grusin).
It relies on fiction to "protect" us from the
immediacy of an actual disaster.

Grusin wrote about premediation in the aftermath of the 9/11 attacks.
