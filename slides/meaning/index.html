Signs are stimulus patterns that have meaning.

They may be reductive, or they may
invite many possible meanings.

Either way, signs have agency.
They, like affect, act upon us.

Semiotics says,
"Don't stop signifying."

Signifying practices are meaning-making
behaviours where people follow particular
conventions or rules of interpretation.

See the MDIA 200 glossary for more, including source material.

Recall three types of signs.

I'm drawing primarily from the work of Charles Sanders Peirce here.

An icon is a sign that
physically resembles
what it stands for.

This photograph of my cat, Tig, is an icon.

This painting of Tig is an icon, too.

An index is a sign that
correlates in space and time with its meaning.

An index points or leaves a trace.

This photograph of fur that Tig left behind
is an index that "Tig was here."

A symbol is a sign that
does not physically resemble what it stands for.

It's meant to be context-agnostic and
work with other symbols in a system.

These are symbols for Tig.

Indeed, signs need systems
to prompt meaning-making ("signifying practices").

Those systems are
called codes.

According to semiotics,
people "encode" signs with meaning for other people
to then "decode."

Sometimes codes are obvious;
sometimes they're latent.

Sometimes they're broadcasted;
sometimes they're narrowcasted.

We'll get to some examples in a minute,
and we'll experiment with types of codes.

Here, for now, are some
challenges for semiotics:

Encoding + decoding simplify the
communication process.

Codes are historical and cultural. IFYYK.

Meaning does not inhere in a work
to be "extracted" by decoders.

Meaning can't be corralled or contained
by codes, either.

Even more challenges:

Do we start to see codes everywhere in media?

Does content become a puzzle to solve?

Who gets to decode? Is semiotics cultural elitism?

And what of decodings we didn't intend to encode?

This last question gets at how signs become difference engines in the play of discourse, where
language is an "unintentional speech" replete with "uncontrollable semantic drift" (see Fry).

Consider three
types of codes.

Here, I'm drawing from Roland Barthes's "symphony of codes" in the book, S/Z.

Hermeneutic codes
(aka enigmas)

"construct moments of mystery to intrigue
the reader or viewer. Enigmas also hook readers,
compelling further reading or viewing to
locate answers to the questions posed"
(Dixon 8).

Remember "Translated, From the Japanese"?

Proairetic codes
(aka action)

"provides explanation or excitement, sometimes
working to resolve the enigmas that earlier
narrative sequences might pose."

"Science fiction, thrillers and crime dramas ...
typically rely on moments of concentrated action to generate viewer excitement" (Dixon 8-9).

Remember Secrets Agent?

Cultural codes
(aka references)

"refers to the inclusion of material that generates
meaning from outside the product."

"in short, anything that relies on the audience's
knowledge beyond the text" (Dixon 9).

Remember THE M00D 0F THE M0MENT?
