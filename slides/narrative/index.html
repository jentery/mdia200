Ok, narrative

Media play significant roles in the construction
of heroes and villains, arcs and perspectives,
plots and subplots, quests and conflicts.

That's the stuff for today.

Narratives can be formulas.

What if I said they move stories from
equilibrium to disequilibrium to restoration
(a new equilibrium)? (yes, three acts)

What examples come to mind?
Anything from class?

I didn't think of this. Tzvetan Todorov did (1977).

Formulas are entwined
with genres.

From equilibrium to disequilibrium
to restoration in science fiction?
horror?
romance?

See Mark Dixon (2020) for more on genre and narrative in Media Studies.

We might think of narrative as something in a work.

a book, film, comic, or tv show's narrative, e.g.

But what if the physical spaces we
inhabit contain storytelling devices, too?

What's the
"narrative architecture"
of media today?

See Henry Jenkins for more on this concept.

Or what is "environmental
storytelling" today?

Hello, Jenkins, for more once again.

This question corresponds with the popular desire
to participate in narratives: to become a character
or player in a world, from amusement parks
and escape rooms to video games, threatres of
the imagination, and live action roleplay.

Let's define our terms
before we proceed, tho.

A story is the "whole tale"
of events in chronological
order, from beginning
to end (Parsons).

The Russian Formalists called story the "raw material" or "fabula" organized by a writer.

A plot is the sequence of
events as unfolded in the telling or narrating of
a story (Parsons).

The Russian Formalists called plot "sjuzhet."
Think of books that start at the beginning or tv shows that
rely heavily on flashing forward and backward in time.

A narrative is a story
and how that story is told, plotted, and arranged.

It "covers both the act of telling or narrating ...
the tale and the tale itself as told" (Parsons).

Think of how people refer to "the narrative" to imply both the story and its narration.

Now think about
Pokémon GO

and specifically its narrative architecture.

It's augmented reality.
It doesn't appear to
have a narrative.

It's something like pure play?

You won't find diaries,
letters, or audio logs
embedded in the mise en
scène, waiting for you
to decode them and
unravel the poké-plot.

What games rely heavily on such embedding?

But it evokes the lore of
the entire franchise, which dates back to at least 1996.

Davies and Innocent: "the property was always intended for spatial interaction. Its inventor Satoshi Tajiri developed
the original Pokémon game and story-verse in 1996 for the then leader in mobile gaming platforms - the Nintendo's
Game Boy. With Pokémon, Satoshi wanted to evoke his own childhood experiences of growing up in rural area
outside Tokyo where he would run around collecting insects in the rice paddies, rivers and forests." (2017: 8).
Image in next slide care of KoS.

It also enacts animations
of chance, where Pokémon
can break free.

Davies and Innocent: "the Pokémon Go player is a map wielding treasure hunter whose activities relate more to the
letterboxing and orienteering activities of the 1800's. Orienteering - like the requisite maps and compasses it employed
- emerged out of military navigation exercises in Försvarsmakten - the Swedish armed forces. This competitive sport
for officers began opening up to civilians in the 1890's and by 1897, the first non-military orienteering competition
was held in Norway (Boga 1997, 2). Meanwhile in England, a parallel activity called letterboxing that had begun in 1854
as an underground hobby for hikers on the moors of Devon, was growing in popularity (Montola et al. 32)" (2017: 5-6).

Pokémon's slogan,
"Gotta Catch 'Em All,"
drives the mechanics.

Narratives emerge from the game, which enables
players to construct
their own stories.

Let's put that claim about emergence to work
by storyboarding a
narrative based on
this scene.

Imagine the equilibrium, disequilibrium, and restoration and then describe them each.

The media constructed its
own narratives, too.

See also "The top five most surprising stories about Pokémon Go ... so far"
and "17 heartwarming stories about people playing 'Pokémon GO.'"

The narrative architecture
of Pokémon GO became an
"authoring environment"
for spatial stories.

Again, see Henry Jenkins for more on this concept.

The irony is that the "the
urban environment simply serves as a backdrop for
play rather than play
responding to the
environment itself"
(Davies and Innocent 7).

Note how the media gravitated toward media effects models of communication to address this issue.

Narratives are often said to transform not only stories
but also their characters.

Who or what would you say
is transformed here?
And how?

To recap: environmental storytelling creates the
narrative architecture for spatial stories and
typically relies on at least one of the following
techniques: embedded narratives, evocative spaces,
enacted stories, and emergent narratives.
